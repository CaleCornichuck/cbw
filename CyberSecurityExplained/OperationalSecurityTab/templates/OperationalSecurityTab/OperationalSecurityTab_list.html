Operational Security (OPSEC)



What is OPSEC?

Operational security refers to controlling access to information, ensuring that nothing can compromise a system or reveal vulnerabilities. This could include information like what emails are associated with a website's users or access to proprietary designs or software.


In general, any information revealed can be used either individually or grouped together to access a vulnerability of an operation or system and compromise it.


Operational security was first coined as a term during the Vietnam War by the team of operation Purple Dragon. The program, started by Admiral Ulysses Sharp, was tasked with investigating the failure of combat operations. Operational Security referred to a five step process created by the Purple Dragon team to be used on existing activities or the planning of new operations.



The Five Steps of OPSEC

  • Identify Sensitive Data

    • Understanding the data used by an organization, how it is stored, and how sensitive it is. This would include customer details, financial records, proprietary research or products, and contracted work.


  • Identify Possible Threats

    • Once the sensitive data is identified it must be broken down into what actually poses a threat to the company. This could be as simple as revealing cooperative business deals allowing competitors to poach the contracted work, or possibly patents being leaked by insider threats.


  • Analyze Vulnerabilities

    • Assesing the processes and systems used to store or secure data, and the possible ways an attacker could utilize them. This could be a hacker using default credentials to access a database or someone physically entering a building to steal patent models.


  • Determine Threat Level

    • Each vulnerability found should be assessed for what level of threat it represents, ranked based on likelihood or potential damage. Other considerations would include the amount of time or work to mitigate or resolve damage. Higher ranked threats will have more resources devoted to their prevention and recovery.


  • Devise Mitigation Plan

    • Once the above steps have been taken, it becomes necessary to devise a plan to mitigate attacks or threats. This would include actually implementing countermeasures to the threats. This could include replacing hardware, updating software, or requiring background checks on employees.
