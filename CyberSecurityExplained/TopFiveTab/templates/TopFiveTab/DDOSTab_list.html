Distributed Denial of service (DDoS)

The way DDoS works is when a cyber attack targets a server or network to overwhelm and disrupt the normal operation. The attack floods the system or networks with requests. This causes the site to shut down or slow down so much the users cannot access. DDoS attacks can happen for financial reasons, political reasons, revenge, or just for fun. Attacks can happen to anyone at any time for any reason. The problem is that the attacks are happening at all.





Internet of Things (IoT) is internet-connected objects, objects embedded with sensors, and objects that can transfer and collect data over wireless networks. Some examples are connected appliances, Alexa, smart cars, wearable health monitors, and smart home security.

A Botnet is an army of computers infected with malware to perform a DDoS attack. The Botnet is controlled by the commander source.



Some famous DDoS attacks are:

  • October 2020, Google posted a blog about an attack sourced out of many Chinese internet service providers. This was the largest bandwidth attack of its time and lasted 6 months and peaked at 2.5 terabytes per second.
  • February 2020, Amazon Web Services was attacked by using a technique called Connectionless Lightweight Directory Access Protocol (CLDAP) reflection. This increased the amount of data sent to the infected sources IP address by relying on vulnerable third-party CLDAP serves. This attack peaked at 2.3 terabytes per second and lasted for 3 days. This resulted in lose of revenue and brand damage.

How to Protect against Mirai botnet attack.






5 methods for mitigation.
  • 1. Anomaly check (check for traffic performance)
  • 2. Blacklist (all access except blacklist members) and whitelist (no access except whitelist members)
  • 3. Authentication challenges (check if user is an actual person and not a robot)
  • 4. Rate limiting (puts a cap on a repeated action)
  • 5. Protocol and application checks
